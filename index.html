The name latte originates from the Italian term 'caffè e latte' which means "coffee and milk". The drink itself has no definitive beginning as several European countries have slight variations of the drink. However, the modern latte was popularized during the 1980s in the Seattle area. Today there are many variations of the latte with different flavours and interpretations, from the simple iced latte to an oat milk lavendar white chocolate mocha.

And with that, below is a step by step guide to making your very own latte!

Pick Your Beans

The main ingredient to any espresso-based beverage are the beans. So make sure to put some thought behind your choice of coffee beans since it’ll create the flavour profile of your latte. If you have local coffee stores that roast their own beans, it’ll probably be the best way go. Beans from local roasteries are typically much fresher than the beans you find in the grocery store.

Calibrate

Assuming you have a proper setup, make sure to properly calibrate your grinder to the right setting. You can also buy pre-ground beans but if you haven’t, you’ll want a pretty fine grind around the size of table salt. Before making your latte, test your dry weights and extract some shots to test the flavour of your beans. Adjust the grind and dry weight to taste.

Grind & Tamp

Once your calibrated to the proper settings, grind your beans directly into an empty clean and dry portafilter. Smooth out the surface of the grounds making sure the surface is as level as possible. Grab your tamper and apply an even amount of pressure onto the grounds. Try to use the same amount of pressure every time you tamp for a consistent shot.

Choose Your Cup

Picking the right cup may seem silly, but the mode in which you drink any form of espresso affects how you taste it. Aside from the flavour, the functionality of the cup matters as well, consider what you plan on doing while drinking your latte, where you are, where your going and if its sustainable. Once you make a decision, preheat your cup by filling it with hot water.

Extract

Dump the water out, insert the filled tamped portafilter into the machine and begin extraction. Extraction should last around 28-32 seconds depending grind, taste, calibration and preferred wet weight.

Steam Some Milk

While your shot is being extracted or after its been extracted, pour some milk into your pitcher. Purge the steam wand by turning the dial for a couple of seconds, turn it off and using a clean cloth wipe the wand. Once cleaned, insert the steam wand into the pitcher. Make sure the tip of the wand is fully submerged and point the wand at an angle where the milk will create a sort of whirlpool. Turn the dial on and keep a hand on the side of the pitcher to monitor the temperature. Raise the tip of the wand out of the milk 2-3 times to create some foam. When the pitcher feels hot or is at your preferred temperature, turn the dial off and remove the pitcher. Make sure to use a wet cloth to wipe the steam wand after use.

Remove the Espresso

If you haven’t done so already, stop your extraction, and remove your espresso. Its good practice to remove the portafilter and empty the used grounds, wiping the portafilter clean, and purging the machine.

Set Up Your Milk

In between having steamed your milk and grabbing your espresso, try to swirl your milk to maintain a consistent mixture of foam and liquid. Once your espresso is set up, tap your pitcher onto a counter to remove any bubbles, you may want to cover the pitcher with your hand when you do this as the milk splashes. For latte art, some people find its easier when the steamed milk is poured into another clean pitcher but that’s completely up to preference.

Make Some Art

And finally, the fun part. There are many techniques, methods and preferences for making latte art but at the end of the day it depends on the artist. Practice makes perfect so you’ll probably be making a couple lattes before you achieve something instagrammable. To make good art you’ll need properly steamed milk and feel for the movements. When your ready, pour the milk into the espresso in a circular motion sort of mixing the milk into the espresso by the way you pour. Take a sip and enjoy your latte! If you want to take a deeper dive into how to taste your latte, consider the acidity, bitterness, sweetness, and umami. Swirl the espresso or latte in your mouth, understand the flavour notes, and how it feels from the tip of your tongue to when you swallow. Examine the aroma, make reference to similarities in other flavours, like fruit or chocolate. Refer to what the package says to what you taste and smell.
